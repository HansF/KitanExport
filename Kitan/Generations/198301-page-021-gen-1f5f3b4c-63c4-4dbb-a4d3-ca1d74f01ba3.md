## Literal English Translation
Ah, first, saying one will strike the belly (get angry), [one] loses one's true mind, and becomes a person who ignores right and wrong. Successively, not getting angry but planning, and executing the strike (punishment), and successively differentiating righteous wrongdoing—if [you] assert that this is the proper standing [for action], then [you] will damage yourself. To think that if the heart has no place for entry of anger, you will not damage the heart of the person, even if you truly damage that person, even if you obtain satisfaction in your own heart, it is not the heart of the other person.

Even from a heart that contains a **summer bug**—what sort of thing did that **summer bug** harbor?—[for a person] to provoke anger in others is something that cannot be hidden even from the fire; and even when read, it is something that cannot be hidden even from the fire.

It is said that piling up numerous deeds and discarding them all, [the act of] criticizing others is extremely mistaken. The stupid person whose heart is empty also criticizes others; how clearly, then, can that person know the failings of others? Furthermore, with the heart used to criticize oneself, it is with that same heart that one seeks to criticize the failings of the person one desires [to critique].

To rebuke those failings, and to forgive one’s own body (self), and thereby forgive others—such a statement is merely **Chū-Nobu Ōkimi’s** saying concerning the disciplining of his children and younger brothers.

If there is a bad deed concerning another person, conceal and hide it well; if there is a good deed, publish and disclose it widely—this was the instruction of **Shushi** (Zhu Xi).

When associating with others, or when employing a good person, if there is something that does not fit one’s mind, the failure to distinguish right and wrong, first getting angry, and the inability to establish [this distinction], this is what the ancients instructed.

You should not [get angry]. Even if others are angry at me, I will not quarrel with them in the slightest, though they may seem to be appeased.

This **Honka (Main Poem)** is something that cannot be suppressed or hidden even from the flame (or fire), yet it has taught us to suppress anger. This writing also, even if it follows the ancient’s poem, there are poems that are introduced, so [one] must not first get angry and fail to distinguish right and wrong.

## Literary English Translation
Alas, when one initially resolves to ‘strike the belly’ (to succumb to rage), one loses their true self and becomes a person unable to distinguish right from wrong. If one then claims that the correct procedure is to calculate the punishment and execute the strike without letting fury take hold, thereby differentiating righteous wrongdoing—if one stands by this assertion, they only manage to damage themselves. You might believe that by restricting anger from entering your heart, you avoid harming the target's spirit. Yet, even if you inflict harm, and even if your own heart feels satisfied, that resolution is entirely separate from the heart of the other person.

Even when driven by a heart that nurses a **"summer insect"**—and what does that ephemeral insect truly harbor?—the act of provoking anger in others is a failing that cannot be concealed, even by fire; and the realization of this, when read, is equally exposed to the flame.

It is said that accumulating and then abandoning numerous errors, the action of blaming others is profoundly mistaken. Even a shallow, foolish person blames others; how clearly, then, can such a person truly perceive the shortcomings of others? Moreover, the intent with which you criticize another’s faults is precisely the same mind you use to criticize your own failings.

To reproach others’ faults while excusing your own, and thereby extending forgiveness to others—this sentiment is merely the rhetoric of **Chū-Nobu Ōkimi** when disciplining his sons and younger brothers.

If there is a bad deed concerning another person, conceal and hide it well; if there is a good deed, publish and disclose it widely—this was the instruction of **Shushi** (Zhu Xi).

When associating with others, or when employing a virtuous person, if something does not align with your sensibilities, the ancient sages admonished us not to first become enraged and thus fail to establish the distinction between right and wrong.

You should not do this. Even if others direct their anger toward me, I must not descend into quarreling in the slightest, even if they appear pacified.

This **Honka** (source passage) itself refers to something that cannot be suppressed or hidden even by flame, yet it has disciplined us to suppress anger. Although this text also follows the poem of the ancients, because it includes poems introduced for discussion, we must not first become enraged and neglect to distinguish between right and wrong.
