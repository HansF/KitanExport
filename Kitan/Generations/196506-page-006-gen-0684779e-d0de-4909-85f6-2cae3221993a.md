## Literal English Translation

**Freedom of Speech and Press**

**Kitan Salon**

Currently in Japan, to publish a magazine or issue a book, no one’s permission is needed, nor is there a necessity to submit copies anywhere. To put it out or to stop it is at one’s own discretion. This seems like a matter of course, but considering the era when one was bound hand and foot by the Prewar Newspaper Law, Publishing Law, and Peace Preservation Law, it is truly a dream-like freedom.
However, the freedom of speech and press in the Constitution stating "No person shall conduct censorship" was not something won by the wisdom of the Japanese people. The abolition of the aforementioned three laws was, so to speak, like a "child of defeat"; if the cold, grim fact of Japan's defeat had not existed, they surely would have continued to exist.
Article 16 of the Peace Preservation Law, stating that when it is recognized there is a fear of disturbing "good customs and public order," police officers may order the prohibition of sale—such clauses would likely have been utilized to the maximum and been very active in the "purification of society."
But, if one thought the shackles and manacles were all gone due to the defeat, that was a great mistake; while the American Occupation forces were occupying Japan, there was strict GHQ censorship. At the time, because we were under Occupation censorship, there was no freedom to put things into print; for magazines, the first issue was "pre-censorship," and from the second issue onward, it was "post-censorship" in principle. At any rate, at least once a month, and sometimes two or three times, I was summoned and given a hard time. The scolding was that I "published an article insulting the Occupation forces." When I wondered what it was, it was a reportage saying "there were prostitutes among white people"; writing that there are women of such a profession among white people is an insult to white people, and an insult to the Occupation forces. Therefore, they said I had published an article insulting the Occupation forces in my magazine.
Somehow, the connection between "white prostitutes" and "insulting the Occupation forces" seemed thin to me, but since arguing would lead nowhere, I simply bowed low in humble apology and was forgiven after swearing never to publish an article saying there are prostitutes among white people again. The reason being, that American officer said, "If you don't want to go to Okinawa and be forced into hard labor, don't publish such articles," and pointed out the window; when I looked down from the fourth-floor building window, inside a perimeter strictly surrounded by barbed wire, Japanese people dressed in old American military uniforms were squirming like monkeys.
Only now can I write such things, but at that time, if I had written this, I would have been sent to Okinawa without question. Eventually, the submitted magazine came back with a slip attached, simply saying "There is no need to submit copies in the future." I recall it was one or two months before Japan became completely independent.
"No person shall conduct censorship." It was only then that I began to feel this constitutional clause deeply in my very bones. Is present-day Japan a paradise of freedom? After 300 years of Tokugawa feudal politics, through the militarist politics of Meiji, Taisho, and Showa, I feel the shackles of bureaucratic control have soaked into the very marrow of the Japanese people's bones. This national character is unlikely to change easily with just ten-plus years of celebrating freedom. However, it can be said that now is a happier era than any in the past.
In order to enjoy this happy era forever, even if I am despised as "weak-kneed," I wish to continue self-restraint. It is certain that a charge trumpet is more gallant than a retreat trumpet. However, I want you to understand how difficult it is and what effort it requires.

**Freedom of Speech and Press — The Editor**

## Literary English Translation

**Freedom of Speech and the Press**

**Kitan Salon**

In Japan today, one needs no one's permission to publish a magazine or a book, nor is there a requirement to register copies with any authority. Whether to publish or to cease is entirely up to the individual. While this may seem like a basic right today, it feels like a dream when compared to the prewar years, when we were bound hand and foot by the Newspaper Law, the Publishing Law, and the Peace Preservation Law.
That said, we must admit that the constitutional guarantee that "No censorship shall be maintained" was not a liberty won through the collective wisdom of the Japanese people. The repeal of those three oppressive laws was a "war orphan" of our defeat; had it not been for the cold reality of Japan’s surrender, they would undoubtedly still be in force today. 
The authorities would surely still be "purifying society" by invoking Article 16 of the Peace Preservation Law, which allowed police to ban any publication deemed a threat to "public order and morals."
However, anyone who thought the shackles fell away the moment the war ended was gravely mistaken. During the American Occupation, we faced rigorous censorship from the GHQ. While we technically had the "freedom" to print, the reality was different. For magazines, the first issue required pre-approval, while subsequent issues were subject to post-publication review. I was summoned for "questioning" at least once a month—sometimes two or three times—and given a thorough dressing-down.
The charge? "Insulting the Occupation Forces." The offending content? A reportage piece mentioning that white women, too, worked as prostitutes. Apparently, to suggest that such a profession existed among white people was an affront to the entire white race, and by extension, an insult to the Occupation. 
The logic seemed tenuous at best, but knowing that arguing was futile, I prostrated myself and swore never to mention white prostitutes again. My compliance was spurred by a certain American officer who pointed out his fourth-floor window and remarked, "If you don’t want to be shipped off to Okinawa for hard labor, you'll stop printing this trash." Looking down, I saw a yard enclosed in heavy barbed wire where Japanese men, clad in tattered U.S. military surpluses, were scurrying about like captive monkeys.
I can only write these words now because the times have changed; back then, such a disclosure would have earned me a one-way ticket to those labor camps. Eventually, my submitted copies began returning with a simple note: "No further submissions required." As I recall, this happened just a month or two before Japan regained its full independence.
Only then did the words "No censorship shall be maintained" truly begin to resonate within my soul. Is modern Japan a paradise of freedom? I fear not. After three centuries of Tokugawa feudalism followed by the militarism of the Meiji, Taisho, and Showa eras, the instinct for bureaucratic subservience is bred into the very marrow of the Japanese people. A mere decade of "freedom" cannot easily undo such a national character. And yet, we must acknowledge that we are living in the happiest era in our history.
To preserve this happiness, I am willing to practice "self-restraint," even if others mock me as "weak-kneed." Of course, it is far more heroic to sound the charge than the retreat. But I ask our readers to understand the sheer effort and difficulty required to navigate these waters.

**Freedom of Speech and the Press — The Editor**

*   **GHQ Censorship:** Post-WWII Japan was not immediately "free." The GHQ (General Headquarters) practiced strict censorship (SCAPIN 339, "Press Code for Japan"). They were particularly sensitive about anything that damaged the prestige of the Allied powers (like "white prostitutes").
*   **Okinawa / Hard Labor:** The mention of being sent to Okinawa for hard labor refers to the "Okinawa construction camps" or detention centers used by the US military during the Occupation. It highlights the absolute power the GHQ held over Japanese civilians.

*   **Self-Restraint (自粛 - Jishuku):** A recurring theme in Japanese media.
