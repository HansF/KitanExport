## 6️⃣ Cultural Notes & Historical Context

*   **Kasu-tori Magazines**: These were cheap, flimsy magazines that flourished in Japan during the occupation (late 1940s). They were named after a low-grade sake; it was said that after three issues (three cups), the magazine (or the drinker) would be finished. They focused on sex, crime, and sensationalism.
*   **The Post-war Struggle**: The characters are "fasting" (likely starving due to food shortages) and the husband resorts to "male prostitution" (男娼) in drag to send money home, reflecting the desperate economic conditions of post-WWII Japan.
*   **Terminology**:
    *   **トアン (Tuan)**: Used here as a respectful address for a master or boss, likely derived from Malay/Indonesian, reflecting the return of soldiers from SE Asia.
    *   **カバイヤ (Kabaiya)**: Likely referring to a *kebaya* or a similar light jacket, again suggesting SE Asian influence/loanwords common among returnees.
    *   **Cafe**: In this era, "cafes" were often bars where waitresses provided "services" beyond serving drinks.
