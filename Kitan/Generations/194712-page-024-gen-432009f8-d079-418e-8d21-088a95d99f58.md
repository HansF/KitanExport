## Editorial & Structural Analysis

*   **Section Type:** This is a classic "confessional narrative" (*zuihitsu* or *shosetsu* style), very common in magazines like *Kitan Club*. It balances a sense of "real-life experience" with the tropes of erotic/romantic fiction.
*   **The "Sidebar":** The right-hand column is a *Kobanashi* (short humorous story). These were often used as "fillers" or thematic mirrors to the main text. This particular one uses the "Hundred Ghost Stories" trope to make a joke about "dandies" (idealized men) who turn out to be ghosts—literally "missing their lower halves" (a double entendre for being impotent or physically incomplete).
*   **Narrative Voice:** First-person ("Boku"). The narrator is a poor, earnest student—a common surrogate for the magazine's male readership, allowing for a "Cinderella" fantasy of being adopted by a wealthy woman.
*   **Target Audience:** Young men, students, or salarymen who fantasize about class mobility and the "mysteries" of wealthy, secluded women.

---

## Cultural & Historical Annotations

*   **Katei Kyoshi (Private Tutor):** In Taisho and Showa literature, the male tutor entering a female-dominated or wealthy household is a potent erotic trope. It implies a breach of social class and the potential for a secret affair.
*   **The Sickly Daughter (Byojaku):** Fumiko’s description (white skin, curved spine) fits the "Ero-Guro" (Erotic Grotesque) aesthetic of the era, where physical deformity or extreme frailty was often romanticized or treated with a mix of pity and fetishistic fascination.
*   **Luxury Markers:** The mention of "Cypress baths" (Hinoki-buro), "Beefsteak" (Bifuteki), and "Coffee" signifies extreme wealth in the early postwar or mid-Showa period, contrasting with the student's "thin" uniform.
*   **Hyakumonogatari (100 Ghost Stories):** A traditional game where participants tell 100 scary stories, extinguishing a lamp after each. It was believed a real ghost would appear at the 100th story.

---

## Lexical Notes & Euphemisms

*   **学生さん (Gakusei-san):** A polite way to address a student, but here it carries a tone of patronization and hidden intent by the older woman.
*   **或る事情 (Aru jijou - "Certain circumstances"):** A classic literary euphemism used to hide scandal, disability, or social shame.
*   **癈れる (Sutareru/Yatsureru):** The text uses a variant of "wastage" or "illness" to describe the girl's body.
*   **色男 (Iro-otoko):** A "dandy" or "lady-killer." In the Edo story, it refers to the idealized, well-groomed male.
*   **腰から下はお留守 (Koshi kara shita wa orusu):** Literally "Absent from the waist down." This is a classic joke regarding ghosts (who have no legs) and a sexual pun (impotence or lack of genitals).
