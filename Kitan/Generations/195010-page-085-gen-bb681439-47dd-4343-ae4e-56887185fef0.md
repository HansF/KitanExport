The time of dawn, when the moon is out, also has charm. This man, having stayed at a woman's place last night, was following a path through the reeds, thinking to hurry home while the morning dew was not yet dry. Just then, because the lattice of this house was raised on the way, as a joke he raised the edge of the bamboo blind (sudare) a little, and found the woman from before sprawling in the same slovenly state as when she parted from the man of last night. "Ah, the man who is this woman's lover must have also, like me, trodden through the morning mist and hurried home," he thought, and the contrast with his own current figure being interesting, he watched for a while with lewd eyes; then, toward the woman's pillow, a fan made of magnolia wood ribs with purple paper pasted on it lay fallen open. Narrow folding papers (tatamigami), whether red or crimson one couldn't tell well, but slightly faded, were scattered at the base of the partition screen. At that time, sensing a person, the woman looked out from inside her covering robe and saw a young man leaning against the veranda. To have her exposed sleeping figure seen by a man she didn't even know, she thought, "Oh no." "It seems like a lingering memory of last night; you are very much taking your time," the man said with plenty of irony, and impudently putting about half his body inside the blind, "I am sleeping like this while thinking of the lover who woke earlier than the morning dew and hurried home," the woman answered, gathering her courage while remaining sprawled. As the man, leaning over, tried to scrape toward him the fan near the woman's pillow using the fan he held, the woman, thinking he was approaching her, felt her heart flutter and drew her body into her robes.
